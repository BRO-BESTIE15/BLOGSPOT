Time, Ambiguity, and Me

Today, my principal accidentally started a philosophical debate.

Well, not intentionally.

He simply asked me:

"What's the time?"

The electronic board was turned on, there was no chalk, and he wanted the time written on the board.

So I answered:

"12:10."

A completely normal answer.

Or so I thought.

The next thing I knew, I was receiving a lecture on physics, units, and how I would get zero marks in an examination because I didn't specify whether it was AM or PM.

At that moment, two completely different worlds collided.

The World of Humans.

And the World of Examinations.

In the World of Humans, if someone asks for the time at noon, standing in an Indian school, with sunlight coming through the windows, everyone understands what "12:10" means.

Nobody imagines that it might be 12:10 AM.

Nobody wonders whether I am reporting the time according to UTC, GMT, IST, or Martian Standard Time.

The information has already been provided by the context.

But examinations hate context.

Examinations want precision.

And precision is important.

A physicist writing "5" instead of "5 kg" deserves to lose marks.

A scientist writing "10" instead of "10 seconds" creates ambiguity.

In science, ambiguity is the enemy.

The funny thing is that if we apply the same standard to everyday conversations, everything immediately becomes ridiculous.

Imagine this conversation:

"What's the time?"

"Please specify the time zone."

"Indian time."

"12-hour clock or 24-hour clock?"

"12-hour."

"Local standard time or solar time?"

"Just tell me the time!"

At some point, precision becomes more confusing than the original ambiguity.

This is where I discovered something interesting.

People often say they want precision.

What they actually want is the right amount of precision.

Too little precision causes confusion.

Too much precision causes headaches.

Language survives because humans constantly balance between those two extremes.

If my principal really wanted complete precision, then his own question was ambiguous.

"What is the time?" leaves many possibilities open.

Which time zone?

Which clock system?

Which reference frame?

What definition of time?

What if I decided to answer like a philosopher?

"Sir, before answering, we must first define the nature of time itself."

Or perhaps:

"The future becomes the present and the present becomes the past. Therefore by the time I answer your question, the answer is already outdated."

Or maybe:

"Time is merely a human construct invented to measure change."

I suspect none of these answers would have improved the situation.

But they would have been entertaining.

The deeper I think about it, the more I realize that ambiguity is everywhere.

Every sentence contains assumptions.

Every conversation relies on context.

Every word secretly depends on information that nobody explicitly says.

When someone says:

"Pass me the water."

They don't say:

"Please transfer approximately 250 milliliters of potable H₂O from that container to my current location."

Because humans are not scientific papers.

We are context machines.

We fill in missing information automatically.

Maybe that's why this incident stayed in my mind.

It wasn't really about time.

It was about the strange relationship between precision and understanding.

A scientist wants maximum precision.

A philosopher questions the meaning.

A principal wants the correct answer.

And I just wanted to tell him it was lunchtime.

Time, ambiguity, and me.

An unexpectedly complicated combination.
