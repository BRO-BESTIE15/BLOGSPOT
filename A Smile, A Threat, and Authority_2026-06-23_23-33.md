The Principal's Power and a Student's Smile

Today our principal came to class to check attendance and talk about its importance.

The discussion was mostly about students who had poor attendance. At one point, he sarcastically mentioned students who hadn't attended school even for a single day.

A student in my class smiled.

Not a loud laugh.

Not shouting.

Not interrupting the principal.

Just a smile and a small laugh.

The funny part is that I was sitting close enough that I could barely hear it, yet the principal noticed it immediately.

At first he treated it like a joke.

Then the student smiled again.

This time the principal responded differently.

He said something along the lines of:

"Don't forget who is standing in front of you. I can make you leave this school right now."

The class became quiet.

But the statement stayed in my head.

Not because I thought the student was right.

Not because I thought the principal was evil.

But because I kept thinking about the relationship between authority and power.

The principal absolutely has authority.

I am not denying that.

A principal can maintain discipline.

A principal can take action against serious misconduct.

A principal has responsibilities and powers that students do not.

But then another question appeared in my mind.

Should authority always introduce itself through its strongest punishment?

If a student throws a bottle at a principal, then talking about serious disciplinary consequences makes sense.

If a student threatens someone, damages property, or commits a serious violation, then stronger action becomes understandable.

But what about a smile?

What exactly was the smile guilty of?

Maybe the principal thought it was disrespectful.

Maybe he thought the student was mocking him.

Maybe he genuinely felt the student wasn't taking the discussion seriously.

Those are all possibilities.

But even then, I wondered if there were simpler ways to respond.

"Pay attention."

"This is a serious discussion."

"Please stop laughing."

All of these address the behavior directly.

Instead, the conversation suddenly became about power.

The student's smile was no longer the center of attention.

The principal's authority was.

That made me think about something bigger.

Many institutions possess enormous power.

Teachers can affect grades.

Principals can enforce discipline.

Courts can make life-changing decisions.

Governments can pass laws.

But having power and mentioning power are not the same thing.

A good judge does not need to begin every argument with a reminder that courts can punish people.

A good teacher does not need to begin every lesson with a reminder that they can fail students.

Their authority already exists.

It doesn't become stronger because it is announced.

In fact, the strongest authority may be the one that rarely needs to remind others that it exists.

Maybe I am overthinking a small classroom moment.

That would not be surprising.

But I still find it interesting that a tiny smile led me to think about something much larger:

The difference between correcting behavior and displaying authority.

And whether those two things are always the same.
