Self-Love Isn't About Choosing Yourself Every Time

"You should always put yourself first."

We've heard this advice everywhere. Social media, self-help videos, motivational quotes. The message is simple: if you love yourself, you'll never put someone else above yourself.

I don't fully agree.

I think people misunderstand what happens when we genuinely care about someone.

When you love someone, they stop being just another person in the crowd. They're no longer an NPC in your life. They become someone whose presence makes you feel safe, understood, wanted, or comfortable.

At that point, your brain doesn't always think, "I'm sacrificing myself."

Sometimes it thinks, "Their happiness matters because they matter to me."

And that's different.

People often say, "If you put someone else's needs before your own, you don't love yourself."

But what if you're doing it because that person has become part of what makes your own life meaningful?

Helping them doesn't always feel like losing something. Sometimes it feels natural because your emotional world now includes them.

That's why parents wake up in the middle of the night for their children.

That's why friends travel miles to help each other.

That's why partners make sacrifices without keeping score.

Would we really say none of them love themselves?

I don't think so.

The real issue isn't putting someone first.

The real issue is forgetting yourself completely.

There's a difference between choosing someone because you love them and believing you have no worth without them.

One comes from love.

The other comes from dependence.

And yes, there's a risk.

When you become attached to the wrong person, those same feelings of comfort and safety can make you ignore red flags. You may keep giving because your brain still believes they're your source of peace.

That's why self-love still matters.

Not because it tells you to always choose yourself.

But because it reminds you not to disappear while loving someone else.

Maybe self-love isn't about asking,

"Who comes first?"

Maybe it's about making sure that while you build a place for someone else in your life, you don't erase your own.

Love isn't a competition between "me" and "you."

Sometimes, the healthiest relationships become a quiet "us."

And maybe that's what people forget when they reduce self-love to simply putting yourself first.
