# The Victim We Pretend Doesn't Exist (all versions)
## Quick read
The Victim We Pretend Doesn't Exist

"When we talk about consent, we say it belongs to everyone. But when the victim is a man, we suddenly start making exceptions."

According to many studies, less than half of sexual assaults against women are ever reported. That's a tragedy.

Now ask yourself a different question.

How many men never report it?

We don't know.

Not because it doesn't happen.

But because many men never even believe society will take them seriously.

---

Imagine a man saying,

«"I was sexually assaulted."»

Now imagine the comments.

"Lucky guy."

"A man can't be raped."

"You should've enjoyed it."

"Bro is suffering from success."

Notice something?

The first reaction isn't concern.

It's comedy.

---

Here's the strange part.

If a woman doesn't consent, everyone agrees it's wrong.

If a man doesn't consent, many people start debating whether he secretly wanted it.

But consent doesn't change depending on your gender.

A "yes" is consent.

Anything else isn't.

It's that simple.

---

One argument I hear all the time is,

«"But what if his body responded?"»

Biology has an answer.

Your body can react automatically to touch.

That doesn't mean your mind agreed.

A physical response is not the same thing as consent.

If someone enjoys it willingly, that's consensual sex.

If someone is forced, threatened, manipulated, or unable to refuse, then it isn't consent.

That's the entire point of calling it sexual assault.

---

The problem isn't biology.

It's stereotypes.

We've taught boys that they're supposed to want sex all the time.

That they're always ready.

That they can't be victims.

That asking for help makes them weak.

Those ideas don't protect men.

They trap them.

---

Some people say,

«"Men are stronger."»

Strength doesn't stop trauma.

Strength doesn't erase fear.

Strength doesn't magically create consent.

---

This isn't a competition between men and women.

Recognizing male victims doesn't erase female victims.

Supporting one group doesn't require ignoring another.

Compassion isn't a limited resource.

Justice isn't a pie that runs out if someone else gets a slice.

---

Maybe that's the biggest irony.

We tell boys to respect women's consent.

And we should.

But we rarely teach society to respect men's consent with the same seriousness.

If consent is a human right...

Then it shouldn't depend on whether you're born male or female.

---

The next time you hear someone laugh at a man who says he was sexually assaulted, ask yourself one question.

If the genders were reversed, would you still be laughing?

If the answer is no...

Then maybe the joke was never funny.

Maybe we just forgot that victims don't come in one gender.



## Deep Dive

The Victim We Pretend Doesn't Exist

"The measure of a society isn't how well it protects the majority. It's whether it protects the minority when nobody is watching."

When discussions about sexual violence begin, one truth is repeated over and over:

Many women never report sexual assault.

That's a serious problem.

But here's another question.

How many men never report it?

Nobody knows.

Not because it never happens.

But because many never expect to be believed in the first place.

---

Imagine someone says,

«"A woman was raped."»

Most people respond with sympathy.

Now imagine someone says,

«"A man was raped."»

Suddenly the replies change.

"Lucky guy."

"A man can't be raped."

"You should've enjoyed it."

"Bro is suffering from success."

Why does the word victim disappear the moment the victim is male?

---

One of the biggest myths is that men always want sex.

Nobody sits boys down and teaches them that.

Society does.

Movies.

Memes.

Friends.

Locker-room jokes.

Pornography.

The expectation that "a real man never says no."

Yes, teenage boys often have stronger sexual urges on average because of biology and hormones.

But having a stronger libido is not the same as giving consent.

Nature may influence desire.

Society decides what that desire is supposed to mean.

Confusing the two is where the problem begins.

---

Then comes another myth.

«"If he got an erection, he must have wanted it."»

No.

That's biology, not permission.

An erection is an involuntary physiological response.

It can happen because of sexual stimulation, while sleeping, after waking up, sometimes when the bladder is full, and even for no obvious reason at all.

The body doesn't ask your permission before every reflex.

The same applies to ejaculation. Medical literature documents that it can occur during sexual assault because of involuntary physiological responses. That does not prove desire, enjoyment, or consent.

People keep confusing biology with permission.

An erection isn't a "yes."

Ejaculation isn't a signature on a consent form.

Consent happens in the mind, not in the blood vessels.

---

Strength doesn't create consent.

A man's muscles don't make him immune to fear.

A deep voice doesn't make trauma disappear.

The ability to fight back doesn't determine whether someone was violated.

Consent is a decision.

Not a reflex.

Not a hormone.

Not an erection.

---

We often compare ourselves with developed countries whenever we talk about progress.

Yet many of those countries have moved further toward gender-neutral sexual offence laws and broader conversations about consent.

Many also include comprehensive sexuality education in schools—not simply to teach about sex, but to teach respect, bodily autonomy, consent, and healthy relationships.

Meanwhile, we still blush when someone says "sex education," then wonder why myths replace facts.

A society that is too embarrassed to teach consent shouldn't be surprised when consent is misunderstood.

---

This isn't an argument against women.

Recognizing male victims doesn't erase female victims.

Justice isn't a limited resource.

Compassion doesn't run out because someone else receives it too.

Protecting women and recognizing male victims are not opposing ideas.

A just society should be able to do both.

---

Sometimes it feels as if "male rape" is treated like a hypothetical concept—not because men cannot be victims, but because society often acts as though they cannot be.

The law is changing in many parts of the world.

Our thinking should change too.

Because consent doesn't belong to one gender.

Trauma doesn't belong to one gender.

Human dignity doesn't belong to one gender.

If "No means No"...

Then it should mean No for everyone.
