We Study English for 12 Years. So Why Can't We Speak It?

If you've studied in an English medium school, you've probably heard this sentence at least once:

"Speak in English!"

Sounds simple, right?

But here's the funny part.

Most of us spend nearly 12 years studying English—from Class 1 to Class 12. We pass exams, write essays, score good marks, and somehow still panic when someone starts talking to us in English.

How does that even happen?

The answer, I think, is simple.

Because schools mostly teach English as a subject, not as a language.

We Learned Everything... Except How to Talk

Think about what we actually studied.

Tenses.

Active and passive voice.

Direct and indirect speech.

Articles.

Prepositions.

Subject-Verb Agreement.

Application letters.

Notice writing.

Formal letters.

Essays.

Reading comprehension.

Grammar exercises.

Nothing is wrong with learning these. They're useful.

But here's what we rarely learned:

- How to start a conversation.
- How to keep a conversation going.
- How to explain an idea naturally.
- How to ask questions without overthinking.
- How to think in English instead of translating every sentence from our mother tongue.

That's a completely different skill.

Real Conversations Don't Follow Grammar Chapters

Nobody talks like they're solving an English worksheet.

Imagine meeting your friend.

Your brain doesn't go,

"Subject... Verb... Object... Present Continuous... Correct tense confirmed."

You just say,

"Bro, what's up?"

Language isn't a maths equation.

When people speak, they don't consciously think about grammar rules every second. They focus on expressing an idea.

Grammar helps, but it isn't the conversation itself.

The Greatest Irony

Many students can write an almost perfect application letter.

"Respected Sir, I beg to state that..."

But if a tourist suddenly asks,

"Excuse me, where is the nearest bus stop?"

Some students completely freeze.

Not because they don't know English.

Because they've never practiced using English in real conversations.

Marks Don't Measure Fluency

Our education system often rewards writing more than speaking.

If you memorize grammar rules and writing formats, you can score really well.

But communication isn't something you memorize.

It's something you practice.

You improve by listening, speaking, making mistakes, getting corrected, and speaking again.

Exactly the same way we learned our first language.

Grammar Isn't the Villain

This isn't a post against grammar.

Grammar is important.

Without grammar, communication becomes confusing.

The problem begins when grammar becomes the destination instead of the tool.

Learning grammar without speaking is like learning traffic rules without ever driving a car.

Eventually, you know the rules.

But you're still afraid to get behind the wheel.

Maybe That's Why...

Maybe that's why so many students finish school saying,

"I can understand English... but I can't speak it confidently."

Not because they're bad at English.

Not because they're not intelligent.

But because for years, they were trained to pass English exams instead of using English as a real language.

Maybe schools should spend a little less time asking students to memorize formats...

...and a little more time letting them have actual conversations.

Because at the end of the day, language isn't about writing the perfect answer on an exam paper.

It's about connecting with another human being.
