NEP vs Me

When I heard about the National Education Policy (NEP), I was excited.

More flexibility.

More choices.

Less stream rigidity.

Students could explore their interests instead of being trapped inside a fixed path.

At least, that was the promise I understood.

Then Class 11 happened.

I originally wanted Computer Science.

The school removed it.

Fine.

Plans change.

I looked for another option and chose Informatics Practices (IP). I was genuinely interested in programming. It wasn't a random subject selection.

Then, without asking me, my subject was changed to Hindi.

At first I thought it was a mistake.

It wasn't.

When I asked about it, the principal told me it was because I had chosen Biology.

"Bio wale IP nahi le sakte."

That sentence confused me more than it disappointed me.

Why?

Because I wasn't asking for a subject I couldn't handle.

I already knew Python basics.

I had learned Object-Oriented Programming.

I knew file handling.

I maintained a GitHub account where I stored my projects.

Yet none of that mattered.

The system had already decided who I was.

Apparently, choosing Biology also meant choosing what I was allowed to be interested in.

That was the moment I started questioning something much bigger than one subject.

Schools tell us to discover our passion.

Then they ask us to choose a stream at fifteen or sixteen.

The moment we choose one, invisible labels appear.

PCM?

Engineer.

PCB?

Doctor.

Commerce?

Chartered Accountant.

Arts?

You already know the stereotypes.

It feels less like choosing subjects and more like selecting a character class in a video game.

Once selected, the NPCs refuse to believe your character can learn any other skill.

Choosing Biology almost feels like signing a blood contract with NEET.

As if every Biology student dreams of becoming a doctor.

As if biotechnology, bioinformatics, computational biology, medical AI and dozens of other fields simply don't exist.

Ironically, modern biology is becoming more computational every year.

DNA sequencing.

Protein prediction.

Medical AI.

Genomics.

Bioinformatics.

Computers are becoming an essential part of biology.

Yet I was told Biology students could not study Informatics Practices.

That contradiction never made sense to me.

The irony became even funnier.

Commerce students could take IP.

A Biology student who already knew programming basics could not.

And while I wasn't allowed to study the subject, I still had to pay the computer fund in my school fees.

That one genuinely made me laugh.

Apparently, I was qualified enough to fund computer education.

Just not to receive it.

Another thing I find strange is how confidently society asks teenagers to decide their future.

Adults constantly tell us to choose carefully because this decision will shape our lives.

Meanwhile, many adults themselves work in fields completely different from what they originally studied.

Even some of my own teachers are examples of this.

Real life rarely follows a straight line.

Education, however, often behaves as if it does.

Sometimes I feel schools have quietly shifted their purpose.

At some point, education stopped being about learning.

It became about sorting.

Sorting students into neat categories.

Sorting futures.

Sorting expectations.

Sorting identities.

The system likes certainty.

Students change.

The system updates slowly.

Students change quickly.

That creates conflict.

This blog is not saying NEP is a failure.

It is not saying every school works this way.

It is not saying every Biology student had my experience.

This is simply what happened when NEP met me.

The school denied me a subject.

It did not deny me the ability to learn it.

Programming does not ask for my stream before compiling my code.

GitHub does not ask whether I chose Biology.

Python has never refused to run because I study Biology.

Maybe that is the biggest lesson I learned.

Sometimes institutions can redirect your path.

They cannot decide how far you walk after the redirection.
